## Gemini Review

# Plan Review: Phase 20 — Specialized Lane Integration and Workflow Compatibility

The proposed plans (20-01, 20-02, 20-03) provide a methodical and additive approach to integrating specialized materials models into the existing operator-governed workflow. The strategy focuses on making `llm-evaluate` the primary vehicle for specialized lanes, ensuring that lineage and compatibility are maintained across generation, launch, replay, and comparison.

## 1. Summary
The plan sequence successfully bridges the gap between general-purpose LLM serving and specialized materials evaluation. **Plan 01** establishes the necessary schema extensions and makes the evaluation core lane-aware by reusing Phase 19 resolution logic. **Plan 02** wires the CLI and provides a real-world proof on the `Al-Cu-Fe` system while ensuring comparison and report artifacts carry the new lineage. **Plan 03** completes the phase with a thin compatibility proof on `Sc-Zn` and updates operator documentation. The overall approach is technically sound, highly defensive of existing contracts, and avoids the pitfall of creating parallel "specialized-only" execution paths.

## 2. Strengths
- **Reuse of Serving Contracts**: Reusing `resolve_serving_lane` and `LlmServingIdentity` from Phase 19 ensures that evaluation follows the same precedence and fallback rules as generation, maintaining a unified configuration surface.
- **Additive Lineage**: Denormalizing serving identity into `LlmAssessment` and `LlmCampaignOutcomeSnapshot` directly addresses the `OPS-09` requirement for auditable lineage without breaking backward compatibility for legacy artifacts.
- **Honest Capability Boundaries**: The plans adhere to the decision to keep specialized models evaluation-primary, avoiding the risk of "paper support" for Zomic generation where models are not yet capable.
- **Proof-of-Concept Strategy**: Using one deep proof (`Al-Cu-Fe`) and one thin proof (`Sc-Zn`) is an efficient way to demonstrate compatibility without inflating the phase into a full benchmarking milestone.
- **Defensive CLI Logic**: Adding readiness checks for `openai_compat_v1` lanes in `llm-evaluate` prevents late-stage provider failures.

## 3. Concerns
- **Missing Specialist Formatter (LOW/MEDIUM)**: The **Research** phase strongly recommended a "specialist structure-serialization module" (Pattern 3) to provide structure-conditioned payloads (e.g., for CSLLM). None of the tasks explicitly mention implementing this serializer or wiring it into the `evaluate_llm_candidates` loop. If the specialist lane receives the same generic JSON prompt as the general-purpose lane, its "real role" may be functionally equivalent to the generic lane, failing to demonstrate the value of specialization.
- **Lineage Collision in `LlmRunManifest` (LOW)**: `LlmRunManifest` is used by both `llm-generate` and `llm-evaluate`. Plan 01 adds lane fields to it. Care must be taken to ensure that a manifest generated by `llm-evaluate` doesn't accidentally overwrite or omit generation-lane lineage if they are ever merged or referenced in the same context (though they currently live in separate directories).
- **Report Surface Metric Mapping (LOW)**: In `compare_patterns.py`, the plan aims to surface specialized evaluation context. Ensure that if multiple evaluations (e.g., one generic, one specialized) are run, the report correctly distinguishes which one provided the `synthesizability_score`.

## 4. Suggestions
- **Add a Specialist Prompt Seam**: In Plan 01, Task 2, explicitly include a step to allow for prompt customization based on the resolved lane. Even if a full `pymatgen` bridge is deferred, the code should support switching between the standard `_build_evaluation_prompt` and a specialist-specific version.
- **Clarify `serving_identity` in Summaries**: In Plan 01, Task 1, ensure the `LlmEvaluateSummary` includes the *effective* model and checkpoint used, as this is what operators will see first in the CLI output.
- **Explicit Test for Fallback**: Add a test case in Plan 02, Task 1 that specifically exercises the "configured fallback" path for `llm-evaluate` when a specialized lane is requested but unavailable.

## 5. Risk Assessment
**Overall Risk: LOW**

The technical risk is low because the plans are purely additive and reuse proven infrastructure. The primary project risk is "functional parity" — if the specialist lane isn't given a specialized input format, the proof of value for `LLM-15` might be underwhelming. However, the workflow and lineage goals (`LLM-16`, `OPS-09`) are robustly addressed.

### Justification:
- **Dependency Ordering**: Correct (Schema -> Core -> CLI -> Docs).
- **Scope**: Well-contained; avoids autonomous campaign execution or Zomic fine-tuning.
- **Safety**: Readiness probes and explicit fallback rules minimize runtime surprises.
- **Compatibility**: High; legacy artifact reading is explicitly tested.

## Claude Review

# Phase 20 Plan Review: Specialized Lane Integration and Workflow Compatibility

## 1. Summary

Phase 20 has three plans across three waves that progressively make `llm-evaluate` lane-aware (Plan 01), wire CLI + compare/report lineage on the Al-Cu-Fe proof system (Plan 02), and finish with a thin Sc-Zn compatibility proof plus docs (Plan 03). The approach is evaluation-primary, additive to existing artifacts, and reuses the Phase 19 lane registry rather than inventing a parallel serving system.

The plans are well-structured and correctly scoped to the three Phase 20 requirements (LLM-15, LLM-16, OPS-09). The wave ordering makes sense: schema/core first, then CLI/compare/report, then second-system proof and docs.

## 2. Strengths

- **Single lane registry reuse.** Plans consistently reuse `resolve_serving_lane()` and `build_serving_identity()` from `launch.py` rather than creating evaluation-specific lane resolution. This is the right call — the research correctly identified that a second lane registry would fragment config authority.

- **Backward compatibility is explicit.** Every plan task includes legacy artifact deserialization as a test behavior. The additive field approach (all new fields default to `None` or empty lists) means existing evaluation artifacts remain valid.

- **Honest about the specialized lane role.** The plans do not overreach — they position the lane as evaluation-primary and do not claim Zomic-native generation capability. This matches D-01 through D-03 and D-20.

- **Two-system proof structure.** Al-Cu-Fe as the deep proof and Sc-Zn as the thin compatibility proof is well-calibrated. Plan 03 explicitly keeps Sc-Zn lightweight.

- **TDD throughout.** Every task specifies test-first behavior and automated verification commands.

- **Config stays authoritative.** The precedence chain (CLI flag > `llm_evaluate.model_lane` > default/fallback/backend-default) mirrors the generation path exactly.

## 3. Concerns

### HIGH Severity

**H-1: No `specialist.py` module despite research recommending it.**
The research explicitly recommends a `specialist.py` module for structure-conditioned specialist input formatting (CSLLM-style serialization). None of the three plans create this module. Plan 01 and 02 thread lane identity through evaluation, but the evaluation prompt itself (`_build_evaluation_prompt` in `evaluate.py`) still sends the same generic JSON payload regardless of whether the lane is `general_purpose` or `specialized_materials`. This means the "specialized lane" is only specialized in which endpoint answers — it sends the same prompt either way.

**Impact:** Decision D-02 says the lane should "prove its value through synthesizability, materials plausibility, precursor guidance." If the same generic prompt goes to both lanes, the specialized lane's value depends entirely on the model being smarter, not on the platform doing anything differently. This weakens the LLM-15 claim that the platform "can use at least one specialized materials model path for a real workflow role."

**Mitigation options:** Either add a task in Plan 02 for a thin specialist prompt formatter that at minimum reshapes the candidate payload for structure-aware endpoints, or document explicitly that Phase 20's specialist integration is endpoint-selection-only and defer prompt specialization.

---

**H-2: Decision D-09 requires "a real runnable endpoint" but no plan addresses endpoint availability.**
The research notes that no local OpenAI-compatible endpoint is running, no GPU is available, and `vllm` is not installed. The plans are entirely offline (monkeypatched). This is correct for CI/testing, but D-09 says "Phase 20 should not stop at a contract-only specialized lane. A real runnable endpoint is required."

**Impact:** If Phase 20 ships with only monkeypatched tests and no operator can actually run a real specialized evaluation, the success criterion "at least one specialized-materials lane is operationally meaningful" becomes arguable. The distinction between "contract-only" and "operationally meaningful behind monkeypatches" is thin.

**Mitigation options:** Plan 03 Task 2 (docs) should include a concrete operator recipe for standing up a real specialized endpoint (even a stub vLLM or TGI command). The research already has this recipe. Without it, Phase 20 finishes as a contract phase with realistic tests but no actual specialized model execution.

### MEDIUM Severity

**M-1: `LlmEvaluateConfig` reuses `llm_generate.model_lanes` but the plans do not explain the resolution path clearly.**
Plan 01 Task 2 says evaluation uses `resolve_serving_lane()` from `launch.py`, which takes `LlmGenerateConfig` as its config argument. But the evaluation config is `LlmEvaluateConfig`, which doesn't have `model_lanes`, `default_model_lane`, or `fallback_model_lane`. The plans need to explicitly state that evaluation lane resolution reads lanes from `config.llm_generate.model_lanes` — the same registry — while the lane selector comes from `config.llm_evaluate.model_lane`.

**Impact:** An implementer might add `model_lanes` to `LlmEvaluateConfig` (violating the "no second lane registry" anti-pattern), or might be confused about which config object to pass to `resolve_serving_lane()`.

**Fix:** Add one sentence to Plan 01 Task 2's action block: "Lane resolution passes `config.llm_generate` to `resolve_serving_lane()` — evaluation shares the same lane registry as generation."

---

**M-2: Compare/report lineage propagation for evaluation is underspecified.**
Plan 02 Task 2 says to add fields like `evaluation_requested_model_lanes`, `evaluation_resolved_model_lane`, etc. to comparison artifacts. But `build_campaign_outcome_snapshot()` currently reads from `bundle.launch_summary` — it has no path to the evaluation run manifest. The plan doesn't explain where compare reads the evaluation lineage from.

**Impact:** The implementer will need to decide whether compare reads evaluation lineage from the enriched candidate provenance (`llm_assessment` block), from a separate evaluation manifest lookup, or from a new field on the launch summary. This is a non-trivial design choice left implicit.

**Fix:** Specify that compare should extract evaluation lineage from the `llm_assessment` provenance block on candidates (since that's where Plan 01 threads it), not from a separate manifest lookup.

---

**M-3: Plan 02 exports `build_campaign_comparison_result` but current code exports `build_campaign_comparison`.**
The `must_haves.artifacts` section for `compare.py` lists `build_campaign_comparison_result` as an export, but the actual function is `build_campaign_comparison`. This naming mismatch could cause confusion during implementation.

---

**M-4: No explicit test for replay identity enforcement with specialized evaluation lineage.**
Plan 02 says "launch, replay, and compare compatibility remains intact" but the test behaviors focus on compare snapshots and report entries. There's no explicit test behavior for replay detecting serving identity drift when a specialized evaluation lane was involved. The replay module has hard identity rules — these should be exercised with specialized evaluation lineage present.

### LOW Severity

**L-1: `LlmEvaluateSummary` serving identity typed as `dict[str, Any] | None` instead of `LlmServingIdentity | None`.**
Plan 01 Task 1 suggests `serving_identity: dict[str, Any] | None = None` for the summary "to preserve JSON output compatibility." But other artifacts use `LlmServingIdentity | None`. The inconsistency is minor but could cause confusion. Using the typed model everywhere and letting Pydantic handle serialization would be cleaner.

---

**L-2: Plan 03 Task 2 verification command doesn't include `test_real_mode_pipeline.py`.**
The verify command runs `test_cli.py` and `test_llm_compare_cli.py`, but Task 1 of the same plan adds the real-mode regression. If Task 2 changes CLI behavior, it should also verify the real-mode pipeline stays green.

---

**L-3: No explicit wave-end full-suite verification.**
The validation strategy says "before starting Wave 2, run full suite" and "before `$gsd-verify-work`, full suite must be green." But the individual plan verification commands only run targeted test slices. The validation strategy is defined separately, which is fine, but the plans could reference it.

## 4. Suggestions

1. **Add a thin specialist prompt adapter** (addresses H-1). Even a minimal one that adds `"evaluation_mode": "specialized_materials"` or reformats the candidate payload with explicit structure fields (lattice params, site list, composition as a formula string) would make the specialized lane do something platform-level-different, not just endpoint-different.

2. **Add an operator endpoint recipe to Plan 03 docs** (addresses H-2). The research already has vLLM and TGI commands. Include a 5-line "how to stand up a specialist endpoint for testing" in the configuration reference or a new operator section.

3. **Clarify the lane registry sharing pattern** (addresses M-1). One sentence in Plan 01 Task 2 action block would prevent the most likely implementer confusion.

4. **Specify evaluation lineage source for compare** (addresses M-2). Add to Plan 02 Task 2: "Extract evaluation lineage from the `llm_assessment` provenance block on enriched candidates."

5. **Add a replay test with specialized evaluation context** (addresses M-4). One test in Plan 02 that replays a launch where the candidates carry specialized evaluation lineage and verifies replay doesn't reject the identity.

## 5. Risk Assessment

| Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|------------|
| Phase 20 ships as contract-only with no real specialist model execution | HIGH | MEDIUM | Add operator endpoint recipe; accept that offline proof is sufficient for the code phase but flag it for Phase 21 |
| Specialized lane sends identical prompts as general lane, weakening LLM-15 claim | HIGH | HIGH | Add even a thin prompt adaptation layer in Plan 02 |
| Implementer creates second lane registry under `LlmEvaluateConfig` | MEDIUM | LOW | Clarify in plan text that lanes come from `llm_generate.model_lanes` |
| Compare can't actually surface evaluation lineage because the data path is unclear | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Specify that compare reads from candidate provenance |
| Replay identity rules break with specialized evaluation context | MEDIUM | LOW | Add targeted replay test |
| Full suite regression missed between waves | LOW | LOW | Validation strategy already covers this; just follow it |

**Overall assessment:** The plans are architecturally sound and well-ordered. The two HIGH concerns (no specialist prompt adaptation and no real endpoint recipe) are both addressable with small additions to Plan 02 and Plan 03 respectively. The wave structure, backward compatibility posture, and test coverage design are all strong. With the suggested mitigations, Phase 20 should deliver on its requirements cleanly.

## Consensus Summary

Both reviews rate Phase 20 as low risk overall and agree that the plan set is
architecturally sound, additive, and correctly ordered from schema/core to
CLI/compatibility to thin second-system proof and docs.

### Agreed Strengths

- Reusing the Phase 19 serving contracts and lane resolver is the right
  architectural center.
- The plans are honest about the specialized lane being evaluation-primary
  rather than pretending off-the-shelf materials models are already Zomic-native.
- The one deep proof plus one thin proof split is a good scope boundary for
  this milestone.
- Backward compatibility and additive artifact evolution are handled carefully.

### Agreed Concerns

- **The specialized lane may still look too generic.**
  Both reviews independently flag that the plans do not yet differentiate the
  specialized evaluation prompt or payload enough to make the specialized lane
  feel meaningfully different from the general-purpose lane.
- **Phase 20 should make the specialized lane more concretely operational.**
  Gemini focuses on proving value through clearer specialist behavior; Claude
  also pushes for a concrete operator endpoint recipe so the phase does not end
  feeling contract-only.

### Divergent Views

- Claude is more concerned about execution-path specifics: lane-registry
  ownership, compare provenance source, replay coverage, and an explicit real
  endpoint recipe.
- Gemini is more relaxed about the architecture and focuses primarily on the
  risk that the specialized lane might not yet demonstrate enough differentiated
  behavior to fully justify `LLM-15`.
